Part 4

Roman roads were built of stone and intended to move army legions on foot wearing leather sandals, as well as being used by wheeled carts hauled by horses. By the standards of a modern motorway or interstate, the traffic was incredibly sparse and slow. The carts that did use the road system over centuries created ruts in the stone, so in important ways Roman roads cannot be considered as necessarily more resilient than a modern road surface. It is beyond doubt that the Romans' road system was and remains a stunning engineering, military and social achievement, but just because a Roman road is "there" does not mean it would be any use as a modern-day road. They have not carried any use more strenuous than hiking for over a thousand years.

Tom Fernley, 2016

Part 5

Modern road engineering supports the ability of millions of vehicles of varying sizes and weights from half a ton to 40-50 tons, with two to a dozen axles, to move at speeds from 1-80 mph (and faster in parts of Germany!), in all weather conditions, night and day. Using a monolithic material like stone would be monstrously impractical as well as stunningly dangerous: a lorry/semi-rig in the rain on a flagstone surface braking from 60mph could skid for hundreds of yards. Building a camber into a Roman-style road to avoid water pooling would require extremely expensive processes for the stone. There probably is not enough quarry capacity in the world to produce the quantities needed.

Tom Fernley, 2016
